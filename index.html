A Singapore AI Governance Concept Map

The Singapore government endeavours to make Artificial Intelligence (AI) a core national technology. Singaporeans will find AI increasingly interlacing in their everyday life. Yet, AI evoked is more than just a neutral technology, instead serving too as a socio-technical imaginary, a method to construct and promote certain possible urban futures.

Hence, this project seeks to map out the concepts of AI evoked by the Singapore government, charting connections between disparate bodies of knowledge distributed across the governmental body's many arms.

A definition of AI

The Singapore government defines artificial intelligence (AI) as "a set of technologies that seek to simulate human traits such as knowledge, reasoning, problem solving, perception, learning and planning, [to] produce an output or decision."1 This set of technologies includes computer vision, machine learning, natural language processing, deep learning and robotics.

[1] Personal Data Protection Commission Singapore. (2020). Model Artificial Intelligence Governance Framework Second Edition.

AI Governance

As the capabilities of AI technologies are further acknowledged, more governments are engaging in AI governance discourse. AI governance discourse seeks to reach consensus on the norms, policies and institutions that manage AI's development, regulation and application. The wide range of actors, priorities, and sentiments that make up the discourse increases the complexity and challenge of reaching this goal.

Over the years, the increasing publication reflects the growing salience of AI governance discourse.

The diversity of government organisations has also been increasing.

The leading organisation in publications of documents (e.g. white papers, masterplans, and fact sheets) is the Infocomm and Media Development Authority (IMDA) (n = 25), while the the Ministry of Communications and Information (MCI) published the most speeches (n=32) and parliamentary Q&As (n = 10).

Central Words

Centrality measures were calculated for each word in the semantic network. The most central words were Singapore, work, technology, data and AI.

Descriptors of AI

The ego-centred network constructed around the word AI reveals the embedded nature of AI technologies situated in their sociotechnical environment. From the network, we can see that AI is associated with other technologies such as blockchain, cloud technologies, cybersecurity, sensors, and Internet-of-Things (IoT). Beyond technologies, associations to various domains of urban life also emerge, such as the economy, work, and community.

Ethical Principles around AI Governance

Ethical principles that characterise the global landscape of AI governance discourse also appear in Singaporeâ€™s AI governance discourse significantly (e.g. responsible, ethical, human, accountable, transparent). Ethical concerns were also present through terms (e.g. challenges, issues, risks) and their associated links (e.g. security, trust). A pro-AI vision of future Singapore is supported by the observation that references to beneficial terms (e.g. good, efficient, safe) outweigh references to maleficence (e.g. bias, harm).

Stakeholders of AI Governance

Businesses, industries and companies surfaced as the main stakeholders in the private sector, from the domains of finance and healthcare. In the private sector, central government bodies include IMDA, GovTech, AISG, and MAS. Persons identified in the semantic network include customers, consumers, citizens, workers, employers, re- searchers, and students.
